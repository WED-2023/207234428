Three Key Differences between web1, web2, and web3:

Web1: This era refers to the early days of the World Wide Web, characterized by static web pages and limited interactivity. Websites were primarily informational, with basic HTML and minimal multimedia content.

Web2: Web 2.0 represents the evolution of the internet into a platform for dynamic and interactive content creation and collaboration. It introduced user-generated content, social networking, and web applications that allow for greater user engagement and participation.

Web3: Web 3.0, often referred to as the decentralized web or semantic web, is the next phase of internet development. It aims to create a more intelligent, interconnected, and secure web using technologies like blockchain, artificial intelligence, and machine learning. Web 3.0 focuses on data sovereignty, privacy, and decentralization.

Explanation with Examples:

Let's consider the use case of searching for "side effects of medications" across different web eras:

Web1: In the Web1 era, searching for "side effects of medications" would likely lead to static web pages with basic information provided by authoritative sources such as medical institutions or government agencies. The content would be text-based, possibly with some images, and updated infrequently. User interaction would be minimal, limited to navigating through hyperlinked pages.

Web2: In the Web2 era, searching for the same query would yield more dynamic results. Users might find forums, blogs, or social media platforms where individuals share their experiences with medication side effects. They could engage in discussions, ask questions, and receive real-time responses from peers. Additionally, pharmaceutical companies might maintain interactive websites with multimedia content, interactive tools, and user reviews.

Web3: Searching in the Web3 era would involve accessing decentralized platforms or applications built on blockchain technology. Users could access verified, tamper-proof records of medication data, including side effects, sourced directly from medical institutions, research studies, and patient records. Artificial intelligence algorithms might analyze this data to provide personalized recommendations and predictions based on individual health profiles. Privacy and data ownership would be safeguarded through cryptographic mechanisms, giving users more control over their health information.
